Accessibility principle

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are built upon four foundational principles, often summarized by the acronym POUR.

  1. Perceivable: Information and user interface components must be presented to users in ways they can perceive.
  2. Operable: User interface components and navigation must be operable; users should be able to interact with all controls and interactive elements.
  3. Understandable: Information and the operation of the user interface must be understandable; content should be clear and concise.
  4. Robust: Content must be robust enough to be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.

These principles serve as the foundation for web accessibility, guiding the development of content that is accessible to all users.

Guidelines

Under each of the four principles, there are specific guidelines that provide a framework for creating accessible web content. In WCAG 2.1, there are a total of 12 guidelines.

  • Perceivable: 4 guidelines
  • Operable: 4 guidelines
  • Understandable: 3 guidelines
  • Robust: 1 guideline

These guidelines address broad accessibility issues and lay the groundwork for the success criteria.

Conformance Levels

To measure and ensure accessibility, WCAG defines success criteria at three levels of conformance.

  • Level A: The minimum level, addressing issues that have the highest impact on accessibility and are the easiest to implement.
  • Level AA: Addresses the biggest and most common barriers for users with disabilities.
  • Level AAA: The highest and most complex level, addressing the most advanced accessibility improvements.

Achieving higher levels of conformance (AA or AAA) indicates that the web content is accessible to a broader range of users with disabilities.

Success Criteria

Each guideline is associated with testable success criteria, which are specific conditions that must be met to conform to the guidelines.

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) have evolved through versions 2.0, 2.1, and 2.2, each introducing additional success criteria to enhance web accessibility. Here's a concise overview of the success criteria across these versions.

WCAG 2.0

Under these principles, WCAG 2.0 introduced a total of 61 success criteria. These criteria are organized into three conformance levels.

  • Level A: 25 Success criterias
  • Level AA: 13 Success criterias
  • Level AAA: 23 Success criterias

WCAG 2.1

Under these principles, WCAG 2.1 introduced a total of 78 success. criterias. These criterias are organized into three conformance levels. Introduced 17 new success criterias focusing on mobile accessibility, low vision, and cognitive disabilities.

  • Level A: 30 Success criterias
  • Level AA: 20 Success criterias
  • Level AAA: 28 Success criterias

WCAG 2.2

Under these principles, WCAG 2.1 introduced a total of 86 success. criterias. These criterias are organized into three conformance levels. Added 9 new success criterias and removed 1 obsolete criterion (4.1.1 Parsing).

  • Level A: 31 Success criterias
  • Level AA: 23 Success criterias
  • Level AAA: 32 Success criterias

These documents provide detailed insights into each success criterion and its application, ensuring web content is accessible to a diverse range of users.

abbreviations of accessibility terms

Accessibility abbreviations are commonly used in discussions about digital accessibility, assistive technology, and inclusive design. These terms help professionals, developers, and users communicate effectively about accessibility features and standards.

How to Use Accessibility Terms:

  1. In Web Development: Developers use terms like WCAG, ARIA, and ALT when designing accessible websites. For example, adding ALT text to images helps screen readers describe visuals to blind users.
  2. In Laws and Standards: Government agencies and organizations use abbreviations like ADA and 508 Compliance to refer to legal requirements for accessibility in digital content and services.
  3. In Assistive Technology: Users of assistive tools often refer to AT, NVDA, and JAWS when discussing software that aids navigation, such as screen readers.
  4. In Advocacy and Education: Organizations like NFB work to promote accessibility awareness, while VPAT documents are used to assess how well products meet accessibility standards.
  5. In User Experience (UX) Design: Accessibility is a key part of UX, ensuring digital content is POUR (Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust) for all users.
