Guidelines and Success Criteria's

Guidelines refer to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), which provide recommendations to make digital content more accessible to people with disabilities. POUR principles form the foundation of these guidelines.

Success Criteria's are specific testable statements under each guideline that define whether a web element meets accessibility standards. Success criteria are categorized by three levels: A, AA, and AAA, with AAA being the most accessible.

  1. Perceivable

    2. Content must be presentable to all users in ways they can perceive. Example Success Criterion: 1.1.1 Non-text Content (Level A).

    • All non-text content (images, charts, icons) must have alternative text (alt text) to convey meaning to users who rely on screen readers.
    • Example: A product image on an e-commerce site has an alt text: "Red cotton T-shirt with round neck."
  2. Operable

    3. Users must be able to interact with the interface easily. Example Success Criterion: 2.1.1 Keyboard (Level A)

    • All functionality must be operable using a keyboard without requiring a mouse.
    • Example: A website allows users to navigate menus and submit forms using the Tab and Enter keys.
  3. Understandable

    4. Information and operation must be clear and predictable. Example Success Criterion: 3.3.1 Error Identification (Level A)

    • If a user makes an error (e.g., missing a required field in a form), the system must identify and describe the error clearly.
    • Example: A login form highlights a missing password field and displays a message: "Password is required."
  4. Robust

    5. Content must be compatible with assistive technologies and different devices. Example Success Criterion: 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (Level A)

    • Web elements (like buttons, form inputs) must have correct labels and roles so that screen readers can interpret them properly.
    • Example: A "Submit" button is coded with to ensure screen readers announce it correctly.

When was it built?

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) were first published by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) in 1999, with multiple versions released over time, including WCAG 1.0 (1999) as the initial version, WCAG 2.0 (2008) introducing the POUR (Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust) principles, WCAG 2.1 (2018) adding criteria for mobile accessibility, low vision users, and cognitive disabilities, WCAG 2.2 (expected in 2023) refining accessibility further, and WCAG 3.0 (under development) aiming to be more flexible and user-friendly.

What is the purpose of Guidelines and Success Criteria?

Guidelines provide general principles to make web content accessible, while Success Criteria are specific, testable rules under each guideline to measure accessibility. The primary goal is to ensure equal access for people with disabilities, including those who rely on screen readers for visual impairments, keyboard navigation for mobility limitations, voice commands for restricted hand movement, and alternative input methods such as switch devices and eye tracking.

In which situations is it useful?

Guidelines and Success Criteria are useful in various situations, including building websites that are accessible to all users, developing mobile apps that support voice navigation, creating digital documents such as PDFs, forms, and reports that are screen-reader-friendly, and ensuring compliance with accessibility laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in the USA, Section 508 for US government websites, and EN 301 549 in Europe.

4. Who is impacted by these Guidelines and Success Criteria?

These guidelines positively impact many groups, including people with disabilities, such as those with visual, hearing, mobility, or cognitive impairments, elderly users who may struggle with reading small text or using a mouse, mobile users who rely on voice commands, screen readers, or zoom features, and businesses and developers by ensuring their websites are legally compliant and user-friendly.
