Principle of Understandable

The Understandable principle in the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) ensures that content and user interfaces are designed to be clear, predictable, and easy to understand. This principle helps users with cognitive, learning, or language-related disabilities, but it also enhances the experience for all users.

Readable (Guideline 3.1)

Ensure that text content is readable and comprehensible by a wide range of users, including those with limited literacy or who are using assistive technologies.

Key Points

  • Use clear, simple language.
  • Provide definitions or explanations for complex terms, abbreviations, and jargon.
  • Include alternative formats, such as audio or simplified text.

Examples of Readable

  1. Good Practice
    • To reset your password, click the 'Forgot Password' link on the login page.
    • Use of a glossary for technical terms, e.g., defining "API" as "Application Programming Interface.
  2. Bad Practice
    • Utilize the hyperlink denoted for credential recovery." (Complex and hard to follow.

Predictable (Guideline 3.2)

Make content and interface behavior consistent and predictable to avoid confusing or frustrating users.

Key Points

  • Ensure interactive elements behave consistently (e.g., links, buttons).
  • Avoid unexpected changes, such as automatic navigation or sudden pop-ups.
  • Maintain consistent layouts across pages.

Examples of Predictable

  • Good Practice: A "Submit" button always appears at the bottom of a form, and its appearance does not change unexpectedly.
  • Good Practice: Links to external sites display a label like "Opens in a new window."
  • Bad Practice: A dropdown menu automatically submits the form when a user selects an option without warning.

Input Assistance (Guideline 3.3)

Help users avoid and correct mistakes by providing clear instructions, feedback, and error prevention measures.

Key Points

  • Include detailed, inline error messages for forms.
  • Use input validation to guide users (e.g., date pickers for date fields).
  • Highlight required fields and provide examples where necessary.

Examples of Input Assistance

  • Good Practice: Labeling a field with, "Please enter your email address in the format: example@example.com."
  • Good Practice: If a field is left blank, display: "This field is required. Please enter a value."
  • Bad Practice: A generic error message like "Invalid input" without explaining what went wrong or how to fix it.

Practical Examples of Understandable in Action

  1. Readable Content
    • Scenario: A banking website
    • Understandable: A savings account page explains, "A savings account helps you earn interest on your money while keeping it safe. Interest is the money your bank pays you for keeping your money with them."
    • Not Understandable: The same page uses technical terms without definitions: "Savings accounts accrue interest calculated based on the average quarterly balance."
  2. Predictable Behavior
    • Scenario: An online shopping site
    • Understandable: After adding items to the cart, the page does not reload or redirect unexpectedly. A small popup confirms, "Item added to your cart."
    • Not Understandable: Adding an item redirects users to the checkout page without warning.
  3. Input Assistance
    • Scenario: A job application form
    • Understandable: The form displays an error like "Phone number must include only digits. Example: 1234567890."
    • Not Understandable: The form simply says, "Error in submission," leaving the user confused about what went wrong.

Techniques for Improving Understandability

  1. For Readable Content:
    • Write at a reading level appropriate for the audience, such as an 8th-grade level for general content.
    • Use tools like Hemingway Editor or Readable to simplify complex sentences.
    • Offer alternative formats (e.g., audio versions, plain-text summaries).
  2. For Predictable Behavior:
    • Keep navigation menus, buttons, and page layouts consistent.
    • Use visual cues (e.g., breadcrumbs) to help users understand where they are.
    • Avoid content that automatically moves, refreshes, or changes without user control.
  3. For Input Assistance:
    • Use inline validation to give real-time feedback as users fill out forms.
    • Highlight errors with clear explanations and suggestions for correction.
    • Use placeholders sparingly and ensure proper labels are always present.

Benefits of Ensuring Understandability

  1. Improves User Experience: Content is easier to navigate, read, and interact with, benefiting all users.
  2. Supports Accessibility: Understandable interfaces are especially vital for individuals with cognitive disabilities or those who are non-native speakers.
  3. Reduces Errors: Clear instructions and feedback help users complete tasks successfully.
  4. Enhances Trust: Predictable behavior builds user confidence in the interface.

Summary of Key Principles

  1. Readable: Ensure text is simple, clear, and supported with definitions where necessary.
  2. Predictable: Maintain consistent layouts and avoid unexpected changes in behavior.
  3. Input Assistance: Provide clear instructions, error feedback, and validation to guide users.

By adhering to the Understandable principle, websites and applications can offer intuitive, user-friendly experiences that accommodate diverse needs and abilities.
